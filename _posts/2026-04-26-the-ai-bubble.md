## "You lied to me earlier, didn't you? Which means you sometimes lie. Which means that it's at least plausible that when you say you're conscious you could be lying..." - Alex O'Connor

The advent of AI was quite inevitable. The hallmark of human laziness can be seen everywhere, from the way we speak mathematical language to text shorthand. We masquerade such laziness as efficiency, and in many cases, it was. But pivotal inventions such as the telephone were made because people felt that it was inefficient to communicate by paper messages, the modern washing machine because 20th housewives didn't want to labor on clotheslines for hours, the list goes on. While all of these landmark inventions do indeed provide a clear gap in efficiency pre and post invention and dramatically increase living quality while decreasing the time it takes to do things, all of these things can pessimistically be said to stem from a natural human laziness. Nowadays in the 21st century, such mentalities of efficiency have been monopolized and corrupted by 'big corp', the alleged circus masters that control our technology. We, as the puppets in this admittedly rather flimsy analogy, are, by proxy or directly, affected by the increasing role technology plays in our lives.

A very common, almost boring example is screen addiction, which is expressed in many avenues. The average American teenager has likely doomscrolled before, and even adults who were privy to times where it was not an option find themselves wasting hours in front of their phones. Figureheads such as Elon Musk and Jeff Bezos are often cited as common scapegoats for this supposed belligerent evil that blasts onto your screens and keeps you hooked. They do nothing about it! some may scream. And like an echo chamber, this festering of negativity inevitably spreads to a large audience, until everybody seems to be embroiled in a classic class struggle (get it?) against the elitists, Musk and Bezos. Furthermore, you may see parents limiting screen time, teachers reinforcing its problems in school, etc. We see screen addiction as a direct threat to healthy life, almost as if it is in complete juxtaposition with it.

Such motifs of society as a whole developing a collective opinion due to the regugitation of information can be seen everywhere. Historically, regimes have used a constant flood of false narratives to demonize enemies; an example is the Third Reich in the 1930s. In the same fashion, an international presence has seen the skyrocketing of AI and viewed it as we would screen addiction, with incredible concern. Their concern is justified - it is the first counterexample to my earlier point about efficiency. The use of AI has been shown to ruin focus, cause poorer study patterns, think less creatively, etc. But this concern, as do all negative viewpoints, spread very quickly. OpenAI had barely gotten off the ground when people were already speculating upon the deeply harmful consequences it could have for the future. These viewpoints, while not incorrect by any means, were quickly circulated. As such, we now seem to hold an extremely negative opinion about the usage of AI, just like we hold a negative viewpoint on screens. We are taught that screen addiction is harmful, and that the abuse of AI for immoral or unjust purposes is also harmful; parents will lecture on it, schools will ban it, international conventions will occur to discuss the future of AI. We typically will not ask 'why have we not abolished screens then? or AI?' because the answer is obvious. They are already too integral a part of our society's framework and our dear 'efficiency'. Except, perhaps for the first time, the use of AI actually has direct negative side effects (many of which are aforementioned) as a price for this efficiency. It seems that I am approaching a point where I will applaud the advent of AI, encourage safe use, etc. But I, like many, am keeping a nervous eye on the negatives of AI. The negatives that effect me most deeply (selfish, yes, but they are quite broad as a whole) can be split quite cleanly into three topics: the delineation from educational objectives of both students and teachers, the megacap of companies causing an AI bubble, and the existentialism of it all.
